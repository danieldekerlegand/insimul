The Irish vs Welsh comparison tells you almost everything you need to know. Irish failed largely because it was imposed top-down through mandatory education, creating resentment rather than desire. Welsh succeeded more because it built from existing communities outward: Welsh-medium schools were voluntary and seen as aspirational, S4C created a Welsh-language media ecosystem people actually wanted to consume, and critically, there were contiguous Welsh-speaking communities where the language had real social utility.

The lesson: people acquire languages they want to use, in contexts that feel natural, with a community that makes it worthwhile. Obligation kills motivation. Immersion and belonging create it.

This is exactly where Insimul's approach has genuine potential — and also where I see the hardest challenges:

Challenge 1: Community partnership, not just technology. The biggest risk is building for endangered language communities without building with them. The cultural knowledge needed to make a Māori or Navajo or Gaelic world feel authentic can't be generated by AI — it needs to come from knowledge holders, elders, and community members. Your role would be building the platform and tools; their role would be filling it with authentic cultural content. This means your most important early work might not be code at all — it's relationships.

Challenge 2: Content depth vs language breadth. You could superficially support 50 languages, or you could deeply support 2-3 with rich cultural worlds that actually teach someone to think in that language. For endangered languages especially, depth wins. One beautifully realized village where someone can live a simulated day in Scottish Gaelic — buying from the shop, chatting with neighbors about the weather, understanding local customs — is worth more than 50 phrase-book-level implementations.

Challenge 3: Who creates the content? This is the hardest practical problem. For Spanish or French, you can rely on abundant resources. For Yuchi or Manx or Ainu, you might be working with a handful of remaining speakers. Insimul needs tooling that makes it easy for non-technical community members to build worlds — define cultural norms, record pronunciations, create contextually appropriate dialogues. The World Management UI you already have is a start, but it would need to be radically simplified for cultural practitioners who aren't developers.

Challenge 4: The culture part is harder than the language part. You mentioned cultures alongside languages, and that's crucial. A language without its cultural context is a husk. The Talk of the Town simulation is actually your secret weapon here — it can model kinship systems, social obligations, seasonal practices, community roles. But getting those right requires deep ethnographic input, not procedural generation.

What I'd suggest focusing on:

Subtract the multi-engine export priority for now. It's impressive engineering, but it doesn't serve the core mission until the core experience is proven. Babylon.js in-browser is actually ideal for accessibility — no install, works on school computers, works in remote communities with just a browser.

Add a "community creator" mode — a simplified interface where a language community member can define their village, their social norms, their daily routines, and have Insimul bring it to life. Think of it as the difference between "we built a world for you" and "we built tools for you to build your world."

Change the pitch from "language learning game" to "cultural immersion platform." The language is the vehicle, but the destination is understanding how a community lives, thinks, and relates. That framing also opens doors to cultural preservation grants and indigenous language funding that pure edtech doesn't access.
